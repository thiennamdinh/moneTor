# Summary of Changes - Monetary Tor Incentives with Efficient Nanopayment channels

## Usage

This file uses the Markdown syntax. For a smooth experience, you may want to read
it in a browser after following commands (if you own a debian-based system):

`apt-get install grip && grip summary_of_change.txt`

Then browse localhost on the given port, or use your favorite markdown reader :)

## Summary

This revision addresses many reviewers concerns and improves on previous version
with more details and discussion. This revision changes various parts of the
paper which raised problems due to ambiguous sentences (e.g., overhead
discussion, the theorem, the priority scheme, etc). To gain space and answer
reviewers' concerns, we removed the further work section (Section 8 on previous
version) as we judged this section less important than change made from reviewers' constructive
comments.

Among the critical concerns, the paper has the following updates: 

  - We rewrote the part explaining our approach to prioritization, and give more details on:
      
      - Why previous approaches based on DiffServ fail on the current Tor
        network, providing further details on the appendix
      - Why we chose to prioritize flux with the flow control scheme
      - Further explanations on the reasons that make us believe that finding
        the right scheme to prioritize flows in various congestion level of the
        Tor network and congestion localisation deserves its own paper.

    Findings are explained in the appendix and summarized in the paper.
  
  - We rewrote the related work (now Section 7) with a detailed comparisons to
    the state-of-the-art monetary schemes, and explain why moneTor deserves its
    place in the landscape for the additional properties it offers.

  - We addressed most of Reviewer A, B, C, D concerns and provide reasons for
    the concerns not directly addressed
  
  - We rewrote must of the concluding remarks throughout the paper

  - We moved parts related to the intermediary sooner in the paper to provide a
    smoother understanding of the design

  - We gave more details in the informal explanation of the moneTor scheme, and
    in the detailed explanation of the Nanopayment channel operations. We hope
    that the reader would be able to grasp the logic of the protocol without having
    to rely on the algorithm details in the appendix.

               ===== Response to authorsâ€™ rebuttal =====

Here is the list of improvements to focus on for the next revision of the paper, extracted from my review and in order of importance:

1. Use DiffServ to provide priority. Fixing the flow control scheme to ensure that moneTor works correctly is fine, but moneTor should be evaluated independent of the flow control change. In other words, fix the flow control first, and then use that as your baseline and add moneTor incentives (with DiffServ) on top of it.

2. Add a cryptographic overhead analysis for each moneTor role.

3. Clarify why the assumptions that the adversary will not attack the nanopayment/micropayment channel part of the protocol are practical. For example, what would prevent an adversary from aborting early (which may in fact happen naturally due to network connectivity issues)? Is there other cheating that could occur?

4. Provide more context about centralized entities. Describe how the centralized scheme in moneTor does not suffer from the same weaknesses as previous schemes. An analysis of moneTor cryptographic overhead from 2. should be used to support your claims (and compare to previous work).

5. The paper is sloppy and needs a significant amount of editing work. For example, many details are missing throughout the paper, claims are overstated, the reporting of some results are imprecise, related work/references are missing and some of the existing ones are sloppy.

6. While evaluating 1., show how moneTor can maintain performance incentives proportionally to the amount paid. Presumably I should get better performance as I pay more (up to some limit).

7. Show why currency divisibility is necessary in the context of Tor, i.e., what problems does it solves that could not be handled by previous work?
